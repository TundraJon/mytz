🌙 Moon Phases

The moon's journey through its cycle

🌍 The moon orbits Earth roughly every 29.5 days — this is called a synodic month.
🔭 We always see the same side of the moon because it rotates at the same rate it orbits Earth.
🌊 The moon's gravity pulls on Earth's oceans, creating the tides we experience each day.
✨ A full moon is about 14× brighter than a half moon due to the angle of sunlight.

It's 3 PM somewhere — time for a little coffee knowledge.

🇧🇷

Brazil: The Coffee Giant

Brazil has been the world's largest coffee producer for over 150 years, accounting for roughly a third of all coffee consumed globally. The country's vast plateaus and reliable rainfall make it uniquely suited to growing coffee at scale.

🌍
Born in Ethiopia. Coffee's origins trace back to the highlands of Ethiopia, where legend has it a goat herder named Kaldi noticed his goats were unusually energetic after eating berries from a certain tree. Those berries were coffee cherries.
⏰
Why 3 PM? Your body's cortisol — a natural alertness hormone — dips in the early afternoon, making 2–3 PM the sweet spot where a cup of coffee gives you the biggest boost without disrupting your sleep later.
🫘
Not actually a bean. Coffee "beans" are the seeds of a fruit called a coffee cherry. Each cherry typically holds two seeds facing each other — the flat sides you see are where they pressed together inside the fruit.
🇧🇷
São Paulo's coffee legacy. The coffee boom of the late 1800s built much of modern Brazil — funding railways, infrastructure, and the rapid growth of São Paulo into the megalopolis it is today. The state's red soil, terra roxa, was prized for growing it.
🇺🇸
By Total Volume: The United States is the world's largest coffee consumer in absolute terms. It is estimated to consume roughly 28.4 million 60-kg bags annually. Brazil follows as the second-largest consumer by volume, at approximately 24.1 million bags.
🌡️
Temperature matters. The ideal brewing temperature is 90–96°C (195–205°F). Too hot and you over-extract bitter compounds; too cool and you under-extract, leaving the coffee sour and thin.
⚡
The Caffeine Myth: Think a dark roast gives you a bigger buzz? Think again! Darker roasts taste "stronger" because they are more bitter, but the roasting process actually burns off a small amount of caffeine. Light roasts usually pack the bigger punch!
